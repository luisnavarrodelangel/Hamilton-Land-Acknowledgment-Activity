Between the Lakes Treaty of 1792 - Historical Context

In 1784, during initial negotiations for the Between the Lakes Treaty, the British Crown gifted the Mississauga of the Credit numerous trade goods including clothing, guns and ammunition. These gifts were often characterized as payment by the British in Historical Records.

About the Treaty

The treaty governing this area is the Between the Lakes Treaty of 1792. It is sometimes referred to as the Between the Lakes Purchase or Treaty No. 3. Consider the implications of framing it as a treaty versus a purchase.

In many Indigenous cultures, including that of the Mississauga, gift-giving and reciprocity are central practices—especially in the context of treaty negotiations. While we have clear records of the gifts provided by the British, there is little documentation of what the Mississauga may have offered in return. Discussion & Takeaway

Discussions surrounding Treaty 3 began nearly a decade earlier, in 1784. The British sought to survey and define the boundaries they claimed to have purchased , aiming to legitimize their acquisition in the 1792 version of the Treaty.

However, the Mississaugas understood the agreement differently — not as a sale that extinguished their rights to the land, but as a rental arrangement .

In their view, the land was shared with the British in exchange for gifts and presents given in perpetuity. For the Mississaugas, gift-giving was a normal exchange practice tied to land access and hunting rights, not a one-time payment for land ownership.
